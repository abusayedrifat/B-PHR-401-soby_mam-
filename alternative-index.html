Slide 02 · Introduction

Introduction to Fertility

🌱 Fertility

Fertility refers to the actual reproductive performance of women. It indicates the actual number of children women have given birth to in a population during a specified period.

Key Point: Fertility = Actual Childbearing Performance

🧬 Fecundity

Fecundity refers to the biological potential or capacity of women to have children — the physiological ability to conceive and bear children, regardless of whether that ability is exercised.

Key Point: Fecundity = Biological Ability to Reproduce

While every healthy woman of reproductive age has fecundity (the biological capacity), not all of them exercise it — actual births depend on social, economic, and cultural factors, resulting in measured fertility.

Slide 06 · Fertility Measure

Crude Birth Rate (CBR)

📖 Definition

The number of live births during a specified period (usually a calendar year) per 1,000 population in the middle of that period.

CBR = (B / P) × 1000
B = Number of live births
P = Mid-year total population

⚠️ Limitation

Uses entire population in denominator — includes men, children, and elderly — making it a crude (rough) measure.

Slide 07 · Fertility Measure

General Fertility Rate (GFR)

📖 Definition

The number of live births per year per 1,000 women of childbearing age (15–49). More refined than CBR as it restricts the denominator to women at risk.

GFR = (B / W15-49) × 1000
B = Live births in a year
W15-49 = Mid-year women aged 15–49
✅ More accurate than CBR — denominator limited to women of reproductive age

Slide 08 · Fertility Measure

Age-Specific Fertility Rate (ASFR)

📖 Definition

Live births to women in a specific age group divided by the number of women-years lived in that age group. Shows fertility at each stage of reproductive life.

ASFR = (Bi / Wi) × 1000
Bi = Births to women of age group i
Wi = Women in age group i

Computed for five-year age groups: 15–19, 20–24, 25–29, 30–34, 35–39, 40–44, 45–49

Slide 09 · Fertility Measure

Total Fertility Rate (TFR)

📖 Definition

The average number of children a woman would have if she lived through her reproductive years experiencing the current age-specific fertility rates. The most widely used fertility indicator.

TFR = i × Σ (Bi / Wi)
i = 1 (single-year) or 5 (five-year groups)
Summed from age 15 to 49
🌍 Replacement level TFR ≈ 2.1 children per woman

Slide 10 · Fertility Measure

Child–Woman Ratio (CWR)

📖 Definition

The ratio of children under 5 years to women aged 15–49. Used where vital registration of births is absent or unreliable.

CWR = (P0-4 / W15-49) × 1000
P0-4 = Children under 5 years (both sexes)
W15-49 = Women aged 15–49

🔍 Use Case

Particularly useful in developing countries with weak civil registration systems — can be computed from census data alone.

✅ Advantages

  • No birth registration needed
  • Computable from census
  • Simple to interpret

⚠️ Limitations

  • Affected by child mortality
  • Undercount of young children
  • Less precise than direct measures
Slide 11 · Fertility Measure

Children Ever Born (CEB)

📖 Definition

The mean number of children born alive to women in a specific age group. Reflects a woman's lifetime fertility experience up to the moment of data collection.

CEB = Dead Children + Surviving Children
Includes all children ever born alive,
regardless of current survival status

CEB by age group traces the cumulative fertility across a woman's reproductive life — foundational for indirect estimation methods.

Slide 12 · Methods

Indirect Fertility Estimation

📖 Definition

A set of demographic techniques designed to measure fertility when reliable birth statistics are not available. Uses incomplete or defective data and applies model age patterns to derive ASFRs and overall fertility levels.

📊 Data Used

  • Age distribution of women
  • Children ever born (CEB)
  • Recent births (past 12 months)
  • Model fertility schedules

⚠️ Problems with Direct Methods

  • Underreporting of births
  • Age misstatement of mothers
  • Omission of deceased children
  • Inconsistent reporting of recent fertility
  • Absence of civil registration systems
  • Incomplete or outdated census data
Slide 13 · Necessity

Necessity of Indirect Estimation

🌍 Developing Countries Context

Many developing nations lack functioning civil registration systems. Census data is often the only available source — making indirect techniques essential for fertility estimation.

🔧 Key Indirect Methods

  • Brass P/F Ratio Method
  • Relational Gompertz Model
  • Trussell variant of Brass method
  • Model fertility schedules (Coale-Trussell)

📈 Why Model Schedules?

Indirect methods apply model fertility schedules that represent the typical age pattern of fertility, then:

  • Apply internal consistency checks to identify errors
  • Use adjustment procedures to correct biases
  • Combine period and cohort data for robustness
  • Produce demographically plausible estimates

Slide 17 · Methods

Birth History Analysis

📖 Definition

A method used in demography to gather detailed information about a woman's complete reproductive history through retrospective interviews — collecting data on each birth she experienced.

🎯 Purpose

Provides comprehensive overview of fertility patterns, especially where vital registration is incomplete or unreliable. Used in DHS, MICS, and similar surveys.

📋 Steps in Birth History Analysis

  1. Data Collection: Interview women on each birth — date, survival status, birth order
  2. Compilation: Organise individual histories into an analysable dataset
  3. Calculation: Compute TFR, ASFR and other fertility indicators
  4. Analysis: Identify trends, patterns, and demographic differentials over time
Slide 18 · Methods

Birth Interval Analysis

📖 Definition

Study of the time elapsed between consecutive births — the spacing between a woman's successive children. Drawn from birth history data.

Birth Interval = Date of Birth (n+1) − Date of Birth (n)

⏱️ Categories

  • Short — <18 months (high risk)
  • Medium — 18–36 months (optimal)
  • Long — >36 months

Slide 20 · Conclusion

Conclusion

📊

Fertility as a Demographic Indicator

Fertility is a fundamental demographic indicator reflecting the reproductive behaviour of populations. Measures like TFR, CBR, and ASFR quantify it with different levels of precision.

🔍

Indirect Estimation is Critical

In data-scarce settings, indirect techniques are indispensable. They transform incomplete census data into reliable fertility estimates through model schedules and consistency checks.

📋

Birth History Analysis

Provides a comprehensive retrospective picture of reproductive behaviour, enabling calculation of period and cohort fertility indicators where registration systems are weak.

⏱️

Birth Interval Analysis

Highlights the health implications of birth spacing — short intervals elevate maternal and infant mortality risk, making this analysis vital for family planning programs.
